How to Livestream a Hockey Tournament
(What You Actually Need)

By Slapstick Hockey · June 2026 · 7 min read

We've streamed hockey tournaments in ballparks, convention centers, frozen ponds, and indoor rinks with ceilings so low you had to keep the camera on a monopod. After enough events, you learn fast what actually matters — and what you thought mattered but doesn't.

Here's the honest truth: simplicity is your best friend. You don't need a broadcast truck or a rack of gear to produce a great hockey livestream. We've done it with two people. The key is knowing what actually moves the needle and not overcomplicating the rest.

The Minimum Viable Crew

Our full production setup runs four people — two on-air personalities, one to two camera operators, and a livestream producer managing the technical side. But we've run lean events with just two: one on-air personality and one camera operator. That's it. Everything else is a bonus.

Volunteers can fill gaps too. If you have someone reliable who can help manage cables, watch monitors, or run a second camera, use them. You don't have to have a full paid crew to put out a solid stream. Work with what you have and build from there.

The Gear That Actually Matters

Camera

We shoot on the Sony FX30. It's a cinema-grade camera in a compact body — great image quality, handles low light well (which matters in rinks), and gives you a clean output for streaming. You don't need to match that camera to get started, but invest in something that gives you a clean HDMI signal and can handle the lighting conditions of an indoor rink.

Streaming Software

OBS Studio is what we use and what we recommend. It's free, it's powerful, and it's the industry standard for independent sports broadcasters. It handles your scene switching, graphics overlays, audio mixing, and direct stream output to YouTube all in one place. Learn OBS well and you can produce a professional-looking stream without spending a dollar on software.

Platform

We stream to YouTube Live. It's free, it saves the VOD automatically so families can rewatch after the fact, and it's the easiest platform for sharing — you send one link and anyone can watch on any device. Set up your stream key and event page at least 24 hours before game day. YouTube has a review window for newer channels before live streaming is enabled, and you don't want to find that out the morning of.

Internet

Use ethernet whenever possible. WiFi at rinks is inconsistent for obvious reasons — you're in a metal building, often with hundreds of people on the same network, and the access points are rarely positioned for broadcast production. A hardwired connection is more stable and gives you the consistent upload speed a live stream needs. Always ask the venue about ethernet access when you're scouting the location. If ethernet isn't an option, a dedicated cellular hotspot is your backup — just don't rely on the rink's public WiFi as your primary connection.

Before You Go Live

Arrive early. You need time to set up, run a test stream, and catch problems before they happen on air. Here's what to check:

  • Run your ethernet connection and confirm your upload speed
  • Get your camera positioned for full-ice coverage and lock in your focus manually — the glass will confuse auto-focus constantly
  • Test your audio levels in the actual rink environment, not in a quiet room
  • Do a short test stream and watch it back on your phone from across the rink — you'll catch things on a viewer device you'll miss on your production screen

The glass is your enemy. Tempered glass panels reflect light and throw off auto-focus. Switch to manual focus once you've found your plane and leave it there. Check it between periods — camera vibration and bumps can shift it over time.

Keep It Simple

The biggest mistake first-time hockey streamers make is trying to do too much at once. Graphics packages, multi-camera switching, scoreboard overlays, live stats — none of that matters if your stream is buffering or your audio is clipping. Get the fundamentals right first: stable internet, clean video, good audio, and someone who can call the game with energy. Build complexity on top of a solid foundation, not the other way around.

A two-person stream with great audio and a steady camera will always beat a six-person production with technical problems. Simplicity wins every time.

When to Bring in a Professional Team

There's a point where a tournament outgrows a volunteer setup — multiple rinks streaming simultaneously, live commentary, sponsor integration, social media content alongside the stream. At that point, the cost of a professional media team is almost always less than the cost of a failed stream that your event's reputation takes the hit for.

If you're running a multi-rink event, need branded graphics and overlays, or want content that lives beyond the stream itself, it's worth the conversation.
