How to Livestream a Hockey Tournament
(What You Actually Need)

By Slapstick Hockey · June 2026 · 7 min read

We've covered hockey tournaments in ballparks, convention centers, frozen ponds, and indoor rinks with ceilings so low you had to keep the camera on a monopod. After enough events, you learn fast what actually matters when you're trying to stream a hockey tournament live — and what you thought mattered but doesn't.

This is the honest guide we wish we'd had when we started. No fluff, no gear-list padding. Just what you need to make a hockey livestream actually work.

Step 1: Understand the Environment Before You Show Up

Hockey rinks are one of the hardest venues to broadcast from. The glass reflects light, the ice creates glare, and the ambient noise from a crowd echoes off concrete and steel in ways that will absolutely destroy your audio if you're not prepared.

Before you commit to a setup, find out:

  • Is there hardwired internet at the scorer's table or production area? This is the most important question. Cellular backup is good insurance, but you don't want to bet a 3-hour stream on LTE.
  • Where can you position a camera that gives you a full-ice view? Most indoor rinks have a press box or elevated section. If not, you're working with what you can mount or tripod-position at ice level.
  • What's the power situation? You need outlets for your encoder, mixer, and at minimum one camera. Figure this out before day-of.

Step 2: The Minimum Viable Streaming Kit

You don't need a broadcast truck to livestream a hockey tournament. Here's a solid setup that won't break the bank but will produce a watchable stream:

Camera

A Sony ZV-E10, Canon EOS M50 Mark II, or similar mirrorless camera with a clean HDMI out is your starting point. If you can get a 70-200mm lens (or equivalent), use it — you need reach for full-ice coverage from a fixed position. A wide lens at rink level will capture mostly stick handles and boards.

Capture Card

An Elgato Cam Link 4K or AVerMedia Live Gamer Portable 2 Plus takes the HDMI signal from your camera into your laptop. This is your bridge between the camera and the streaming software.

Encoding Software

OBS Studio is free, powerful, and what most broadcasters use. Set your output to 1080p at 30fps minimum, with a bitrate of 4,500–6,000 kbps if your upload speed allows it. For a multi-rink tournament, you'll need a separate encoder and laptop for each simultaneous stream.

Audio

This is where most tournament streams fall apart. On-camera microphones pick up everything — the Zamboni, the announcer from three rinks over, a toddler three rows back. You have two real options:

  • A wired shotgun mic (like the Rode VideoMicro) mounted on your camera, pointed at the ice
  • A direct feed from the rink's PA system into your mixer, if they have one and are willing to share it

Option 2 is almost always better. Ask the tournament organizer if they have a PA board and whether you can tap it. Most are happy to help.

Internet

You need a minimum upload speed of 10 Mbps for a 1080p stream. 20+ is comfortable. Always run a speed test at the venue before the event, not in your office the week before. Rink WiFi is often unreliable — ask about hardwired ethernet access or bring a cellular hotspot as backup.

Step 3: Choose Your Streaming Platform

YouTube Live is the default choice for most tournament coverage and for good reason — it's free, it saves the VOD automatically, and it's easy for families to share the link. Facebook Live is a close second for community-built tournaments where most of the audience is already in a Facebook group. Twitch works if you have a gaming-adjacent audience but isn't where most hockey families naturally land.

Pro tip: Set up your YouTube stream key and event page at least 24 hours in advance. YouTube has a review window for new channels before they can go live, and nothing is worse than showing up game-day and finding out you're not approved yet.

Step 4: On the Day — What to Expect

Plan to arrive at least 90 minutes before the first puck drop. You need time to:

  • Run your internet speed test and set up the hardwire if available
  • Position and focus your camera (the glass will affect auto-focus — manual is better)
  • Test your audio levels with the rink's ambient noise
  • Do a 5-minute test stream and check it on a phone from across the rink

The test stream from a second device is critical. You will miss things on your encoder screen that are obviously broken when you actually watch the stream. Encoding artifacts, audio sync issues, and framing problems all show up immediately on a viewer device.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Relying entirely on WiFi. Always have a cellular backup. Always.
  • Auto-focus through the glass. The tempered glass panels will confuse your camera constantly. Switch to manual focus once you've found your plane.
  • Not accounting for rink lighting. Many arena lighting setups are designed for the ice surface, not for cameras. Bring a color profile that handles the green/yellow fluorescent cast, or plan to correct in post.
  • Forgetting to monitor audio throughout. Check your levels between periods. Crowd noise and PA systems shift as the arena fills and empties.
  • One person doing everything. Cameras, audio, stream monitoring, and commentary is a four-person job. Start with just video and audio on the stream — commentary can come later when you have the bandwidth.

When to Just Hire Someone

There's a point where the complexity of a multi-rink tournament with simultaneous streams and live commentary is genuinely beyond a volunteer setup — and that's okay. Professional hockey media teams exist specifically for this, and the cost of hiring one is almost always less than the cost of a failed stream that your tournament's reputation takes the hit for.

If you're running more than one rink simultaneously, need live commentary, want social media content alongside the stream, or are hosting a high-profile event, consider bringing in a dedicated media team rather than DIYing it with borrowed gear and volunteers.
